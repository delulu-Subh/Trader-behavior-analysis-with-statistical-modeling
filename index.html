An independent quantitative study examining how Bitcoin Fear & Greed sentiment cycles correlate with per-trade profitability, directional bias, and execution consistency across Hyperliquid perpetual futures traders. Key finding: Fear-regime trades are associated with statistically higher average PnL than Greed-regime trades (p<0.001). Results are exploratory and non-causal.

02 · Methodology
Data Sources & Analytical Approach

A concise summary of how data was collected, how sentiment was matched to trades, and which statistical methods were applied. Full code is available on GitHub.

Trade data was collected via the Hyperliquid public API, which exposes closed perpetual futures trade records including entry/exit price, realized PnL, position size, and direction (long/short). Traders were selected by querying the public leaderboard for accounts with a minimum activity threshold of 500 closed trades during the study window (December 2023 – May 2025), yielding 32 qualifying addresses. No filtering by profitability was applied during selection; however, the sample reflects only active accounts, introducing survivorship bias (see Section 03).

Sentiment data was sourced from the Alternative.me Fear & Greed Index, a composite daily score (0–100) aggregating volatility, market momentum, social volume, dominance, and Google Trends signals for Bitcoin. Each closed trade was assigned the daily sentiment score corresponding to its close date, then classified into five regimes: Extreme Fear (0–24), Fear (25–44), Neutral (45–54), Greed (55–74), and Extreme Greed (75–100).

Statistical methods applied: Exploratory Data Analysis (group summary statistics, distribution plots), one-way ANOVA to test whether mean PnL differs significantly across the five sentiment regimes, and Welch t-tests for pairwise regime comparisons (Welch's correction used because group variances are unequal). A Logistic Regression model was trained on trade-level features — sentiment encoding, position size, and direction — to predict trade outcome (win/loss), with ROI explicitly excluded to prevent target leakage. Model performance was validated via 5-fold stratified cross-validation.

03 · Scope & Limitations
What This Analysis Can & Cannot Conclude

These constraints are stated transparently before any claims. Acknowledging limitations is a feature of credible research, not a weakness. All findings must be interpreted in this context.

Small Trader Sample (n=32)
Only 32 unique traders are represented. Statistical significance is established within this sample, but results cannot be generalized to the broader retail or institutional population. Findings are exploratory, not confirmatory.
Survivorship Bias
All 32 traders were actively trading at the time of data collection. Traders who suffered account blow-ups or became inactive during the study window are entirely absent. This inflates reported win rates and average PnL figures across all regimes.
No Causal Inference
Sentiment regime is associated with trade performance in this dataset, but association is not causation. Third variables (e.g., market volatility, BTC price trend, trader learning effects over time) may explain the observed differences and are not controlled for in this analysis.
No Per-Trade Leverage Data
Leverage is unavailable at the individual trade level, making true risk-adjusted return analysis impossible. Any Sharpe-like metric used is a PnL/volatility proxy — treat it as directionally indicative only, not as a rigorous risk measure.
Single Venue (Hyperliquid)
All data originates from Hyperliquid perpetuals. Funding rate structures, liquidity depth, and order flow characteristics differ across venues. Findings may not replicate on Binance, Bybit, dYdX, or other platforms.
Single Asset Focus
The Fear & Greed Index is Bitcoin-specific. While traders on Hyperliquid trade multiple assets, sentiment classification is based solely on BTC sentiment. Cross-asset validity of this mapping is untested in this study.

Important: This is observational, cross-sectional analysis. No experiment was conducted and no causal mechanism is established. All findings describe patterns observed in a specific dataset over a specific time window. They should not be interpreted as predictive rules or investment guidance.

04 · Exploratory Data Analysis
Sentiment-Grouped Performance

All key metrics broken down by sentiment classification. The primary pattern — Fear regimes associated with higher avg PnL — is consistent across multiple metrics. Figures are within-sample; survivorship bias applies throughout.

Pattern
Fear-period trades show the highest observed avg PnL ($126) and win rate (88.6%). Extreme Greed yields the lowest avg PnL ($46) despite a relatively high 87.4% win rate — driven by compressed position sizes and tighter outcome distributions.
Possible Interpretation
Market stress episodes may select for higher-conviction participants, as lower-skilled or momentum-driven traders reduce activity during Fear regimes. This would leave a sharper concentration of disciplined operators — but this mechanism is speculative and untested here.
Observation
Greed shows the lowest win rate (76.1%) of any regime. The combination of elevated sentiment and lower execution quality is statistically consistent and robust across Welch t-tests (see Section 07). Mechanism is not established by this data alone.

06 · Positioning Analysis
Long vs Short Dynamics by Sentiment

How sentiment correlates with directional bias in this cohort. Note: observed directional patterns in a 32-trader sample cannot be extrapolated as general trading strategies.

Pattern
Long ratio falls from 66.7% (Extreme Fear) to 41.4% (Greed). This cohort shifts toward net-short positioning as sentiment rises — a pattern consistent with contrarian orientation, though not causal evidence of it.
Counterintuitive Finding
Despite the cohort's short-side lean, long positions during Greed outperform long positions during Fear on an ROI basis (3.21% vs 1.90%), which is statistically significant (p<0.0001). Price momentum appears to be a real factor on this venue during elevated sentiment periods.
Observation
Short-side ROI peaks during Fear at 6.67% — the highest of any sentiment/direction combination in this dataset. Whether this pattern reflects skill, luck, or market structure during the study window cannot be determined from this analysis.

08 · Machine Learning
Win Prediction Model — Logistic Regression

Binary classification of trade outcome (win/loss) using only independent features: sentiment regime, position size, and trade direction. ROI is deliberately excluded. Model validated via 5-fold stratified cross-validation.

🔬

Tree-based models were excluded due to data leakage producing unrealistic performance. Random Forest and XGBoost achieved near-perfect scores during initial evaluation because the ROI feature — when included — is deterministically linked to the win/loss label (positive ROI by definition equals a win). This constitutes target leakage, not predictive signal. All excluded models and this decision are documented in the project notebook on GitHub. The Logistic Regression below was retrained with ROI excluded, making it the only model that reflects genuine signal from independent variables.

09 · Key Findings & Strategy Interpretation
Behavioral Patterns Observed in This Dataset

Six consistent patterns observed in the data, followed by a regime-specific interpretation table. These are descriptive findings, not prescriptive rules. Trading decisions based solely on these patterns carry substantial risk and are not recommended.

Disclaimer: The following section describes patterns observed in a 32-trader survivorship-biased dataset on a single venue over a specific time window. This is observational analysis, not trading advice. Past patterns in a limited sample do not predict future market behavior. All "action" language has been replaced with historical pattern descriptions.

📉
Fear Regimes Associated with Higher PnL
Average PnL during Fear was $126.41 per trade vs $46.23 during Extreme Greed — a 2.73× differential confirmed statistically (p<0.001, Welch t-test). This pattern is consistent within this sample but may not generalize.
⚠️
Greed Regimes Historically Associated with Lower Win Rate
Win rate during Greed was 76.1% — the lowest observed regime — despite a broadly optimistic market backdrop. This cohort's crowded entries and later positioning may explain compressed per-trade outcomes, though this is not confirmed.
🔄
Long-Side Momentum Persists in Greed Periods
Long trades during Extreme Greed yielded 2.59% avg ROI vs 1.71% during Extreme Fear (p<0.0001). Within this dataset, momentum on the long side was more pronounced during elevated sentiment — a finding that challenges simple contrarianism.
🎯
Short-Side Historically Strongest During Fear
Short trades during Fear generated 6.67% avg ROI — the highest of any sentiment/direction combination in this dataset. This represents the primary observed return source for the top-performance tier, though it may reflect the specific market conditions of the study window.
👑
Top-Tier Traders Show Regime-Independent Win Rates
The top 7 traders (by cumulative PnL) maintained >93% win rate across all five sentiment regimes. The bottom 25 ranged from 73% to 87%. This consistency is consistent with systematic, rule-based execution — but the sample is too small for strong conclusions.
📊
Directional Bias Inverts at Elevated Sentiment
Long ratio fell from 66.7% in Extreme Fear to 41.4% in Greed. This cohort systematically shifted to net-short positioning as sentiment rose — a structural pattern in this dataset that may reflect deliberate strategy, self-selection, or the specific market trajectory during 2024–2025.
